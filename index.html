Play Distributions and Creating a 'Balanced' Offense

Observing a team's playcall tendencies is one of the most powerful advantages analytics can offer in sports. It's very common for coaches around the league to talk at length about the importance of balance when it comes to running or passing the football, often refferring to an even split between the two. But is this what a balanced offense actually means? WSU Cougars head coach Mike Leach spoke at the recent MIT Sloan sports analytics convention, and had the following to say when asked about the idea of "balance":

"I want all of the positions to touch it," Leach said. "There's nothing balanced about 50 percent run, 50 percent pass, because that's 50 percent stupid."

Leach's point is essentially an offense can only be balanced if each skill position player has the same amount of touches. This is basically the concept of the 'Spread Offense', short quick passes with the goal of exploiting space. The despute seems to be over the definition of balance, but not whether or not balance would be beneficial. I chose to investigate playcalling distributions to see whether or not there was a an influence on a teams success due to how often they decided to run or pass. In the 2018 season, the average net yards per (passing) attempt was 6.4, yet the average yards per rushing attempt was a whole 2 yards lower, at 2.4. Below are all 32 teams play distributions 2018.

When creating these play distribution charts, I chose to only include Passes, Runs and Sacks, as these are the outcomes that can teach us about playcalling tendencies. Every sack would occur on a attempted pass play, so the combined percentages of passes and sacks gives us a clearer impression of the true Pass/Run split.

Expected Points Added per Play

Expected Points Added per Play is a great way to evaluate the most prolific offenses in the league. Teams with a high EPA/Play are not necessarily good teams, as this is mostly an offensive metric. However, it does provide an insight into the strengths and weaknesses of teams. For example, the Atlanta Falcons are fourth in average Expected Points Added per Play, yet sit at twentieth in my overall ELO rankings. This suggests that whilst they are an elite offensive team, defense and special teams could be holding them back.

Understanding Variance

In order to understand the variance from league average, I created variance graphs for each of the three play types in the play distributions. Variance can give us more information than just observing the mean percentages. For instance, when looking at sack variance, we can see that the five teams that are the best at avoiding sacks (Indianapolis, New England, New Orleans, Pittsburgh and Kansas City) are all comfortably in the top 10 in Expected Points Added per Play, as seen above.
